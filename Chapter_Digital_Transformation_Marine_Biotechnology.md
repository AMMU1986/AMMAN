## Abstract

The convergence of digital technologies with marine biotechnology is reshaping the seaweed industry from cultivation through bioprocessing to end-product delivery, enabling unprecedented levels of efficiency, sustainability, and traceability [1, 2]. This chapter examines the digital transformation of marine biotechnology through the lens of Industry 5.0, which emphasizes human-centric design, resilience, and sustainability as core principles guiding technological integration [3, 4]. The enabling digital technologies reviewed include artificial intelligence and machine learning for predictive analytics and process optimization, Internet of Things (IoT) sensor networks for real-time environmental monitoring, digital twin platforms for virtual simulation and scenario testing, robotics and automation for precision harvesting, blockchain for supply chain transparency, and big data analytics for decision support [5, 6, 7]. Applications spanning precision seaweed cultivation, intelligent biorefinery systems, quality control, and circular bioeconomy integration are critically assessed [8, 9]. The chapter further addresses technical, economic, and regulatory challenges alongside cybersecurity and data governance considerations, before concluding with future perspectives on autonomous marine systems, generative AI, and digital ecosystem development [10, 11, 12].

**Keywords:** Marine biotechnology; Seaweed engineering; Digital transformation; Industry 5.0; Artificial intelligence; IoT; Digital twins; Blockchain; Smart aquaculture; Circular bioeconomy

## 1. Foundations of Digital Transformation in Marine Biotechnology

### 1.1. Introduction to Marine Biotechnology and Seaweed Engineering

Marine biotechnology is an area of biotechnology that involves using of living organisms or processes found in the sea to create products and services in healthcare, foods, energy, materials and environmental management [1, 13]. Seaweed (macroalgae) cultivation and processing is one of the fastest growing sectors of the blue economy with global cultivation of seaweed now exceeding 35 million tonnes (worth around USD 16.5 billion) and the potential to increase to 50 million tonnes million by 2030 under the drive of demand for such as food ingredients, phycocolloids (agar, carrageenan, alginate), biofuels, nutraceuticals, cosmetics and bioplastics [2, 14]. The seaweed industry includes a large value chain from selecting seaweed strains and developing seaweed seed stocks, to seaweed cultivation (offshore, nearshore, and land-based), harvesting, primary processing (drying and milling), biorefinery extraction of multiple seaweed products, and final formulation and delivery of products [3, 15].

The underlying complexity of the various seaweed cultivation systems is also crucial, as they are subject to dynamic environmental factors (light penetration, wave energy, biofouling, temperature and salinity) and biological variability (growth rate and biochemical composition) alongside logistical constraints (remote site access, seasonal harvesting and perishable biomass) which makes the integration of digital technology all the more compelling [4, 16]. Traditional management methods based on periodic manual observation and experience-based decisions are now proving to be less convenient for optimizing production at commercial scale, especially when the Company starts exploring more challenging offshore environment and the need for producing higher quality product for the pharmaceutical and food-grade applications is getting increasingly stringent and demanding [5, 17]. Adding to this is increasingly variable seasonal cycles and climate change effects on ocean ecosystems, such as changing nutrient patterns, ocean warming and ocean acidification, which change historical realities that traditional ways of farming depend on [18, 6]. Seaweed engineering is poised to overcome these challenges with the digital transformation, facilitated by a continuous monitoring, data-driven optimization, predictive maintenance and intelligent automation throughout the value chain [6, 18].

### 1.2. Digital Transformation in the Blue Economy

Digital transformation in the blue economy is the strategic use of computing technologies, data analytics, and networked systems throughout marine industries to generate new value, gain efficiency, and promote environmental sustainability [7, 19]. With the onset of the Fourth Industrial Revolution (Industry 4.0), Cyber-Physical Systems, IoT connectivity, cloud computing, and autonomous systems were being introduced in the manufacturing and agricultural sectors and they now need to be adapted for the specific requirements of marine applications, such as resistance to salt water corrosion, underwater communications difficulties, remote powering, and withstand harsh weather conditions [8, 20]. By 2028, the market is expected to top USD 3.5 billion worldwide, as the use of digital solutions in aquaculture is increasingly acknowledged to have the potential to achieve all three, enhancing productivity, lowering the environmental footprint, and guaranteeing food safety in marine production systems [9, 21].

The deployment of digital technology to the marine environment is faced with challenges that make it unique from its application to land-based environments [10, 22]. Scaling constraints imposed by saltwater corrosion, hydrostatic pressure, biological fouling, extreme wave forces, limited access for system maintenance and absence of terrestrial communication and power infrastructure result in cost and complexities to the systems relative to their land based counterparts [11, 23]. Due to the limited range of available communication modalities (acoustic, optical or electromagnetic), all with orders of magnitude lower data throughput than terrestrial wireless, underwater wireless sensors are severely constrained in the data throughput they can return from beneath the surface [12, 24]. These constraints lead to innovation in edge computing (data processing in the local area to minimise communication requirements), energy harvesting (wave, solar and tidal power for autonomous sensor nodes) and robust system design (addition of redundant sensors and self-diagnostic features, graceful degradation under component failure) which would eventually be transferred to terrestrial applications as well [13, 25].

The Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0 paradigm shift is a different philosophy, in which the focus is on human-centric innovation in which employees are creative partners, not displaced jobs, in the technological automation process [10, 22]. The Industry 5.0 framework for marine biotechnology is a network that brings together five pillars: human-machine collaboration, sustainable bioprocessing, resilient supply chains, personalized products, and circular resource management; all intertwined in a digital network with real-time communication and adaptive decision-making capability as illustrated in Figure 1 [11, 23]. Essentially, this framework assumes that marine ecosystems are complex adaptive systems, which cannot be dealt with by black and white thinking, one-size fits all solutions, but in a context sensitive manner, i.e., using flexible solutions in combination with human expertise and computational intelligence [12, 24].

### 1.3. Industry 5.0: Principles and Human-Centric Manufacturing

The Industry 5.0 concept, as formulated by the European Commission, extends the current notion of Industry 4.0 by introducing the 3 values described below [13, 25]: As part of the marine biotechnology domain, human-centricity can be interpreted as the capacity to serve as means of augmenting capabilities of marine farmers, bioprocess engineers and quality specialists by leveraging their expertise through decision-support systems, collaborative robotics (cobots), and intuitive interfaces that do not replace domain knowledge [14, 26]. Sustainability demands that digital technologies have to be proven to reduce the environmental impact of seaweed production and processing, that they can contribute to the attainment of the goal of 'net-zero carbon', that they are energy-saving and that they allow cycling the material flows [15, 27].

The resilience of systems within marine biotechnology refers to the ability to avoid production interruptions and product quality losses due to climate change, disease outbreaks and disruption of supply chains and/or laws and regulations [16, 28]. Modifications of products' processes, using technologies like PC and digitalisation of supply chains, that allows real time information on geographically distributed processes, contribute to resilience by reducing dependency on specific production sites, assessing environmental stress and giving a warning before a crop failure [17, 29]. The Industry 5.0 paradigm also highlights that technological innovations should be guided by Responsible Innovation principles, such as stakeholder engagement, environmental justice, as well as distribution of benefits to all coastal communities whose livelihoods are dependent on marine resources [18, 30]. ### 1.4. Role of Digital Technologies in Sustainable Seaweed Bioprocessing

Digital technologies can support and enable the sustainability of seaweed bioprocessing in a number of ways, including by reducing resource use and waste generation, by enhancing the circularity of such processes and by supporting the transparent environmental reporting of processes [20, 32]. Environmental 'hot spots' from life cycle assessments (LCAs) of conventional seaweed processing suggest that drying requires 30-50% of the total processing energy and that energy is a significant 'hot spot' due to the amount of chemicals used in extraction phases which can be greatly reduced by intelligent process control and optimisation [21, 33]. AI-driven drying routines can cut energy use by 15-30% over fixed parameters drying and are also able to adjust drying routines to maintain product quality consistency depending on moisture levels, ambient humidity, and energy costs [22, 34].

Digital technologies, when combined with the concept of seaweed biorefinery, can make it possible to extract several products streams such as proteins, polysaccharides, lipids, pigments and minerals from the same biomass feedstock in cascaded processes to maximize value recovery whilst minimizing waste [23, 35]. Depending on the incoming biomass composition (that fluctuates both seasonally and between different species) the market requirement for different products, the distribution of available renewable energy and the valorization opportunities that can be created out of the waste streams, intelligent scheduling algorithms can dynamically adjust the biorefinery operation [24, 36]. Additionally, digital systems allow for quantifying and verifying ecosystem services that can arise from seaweed farming, such as carbon sequestration (1.5-5.0 tonnes CO2/ha/year), uptake of the nutrients nitrogen and phosphorus from eutrophic waters, or provision of habitats for marine biodiversity, which can be monetized as carbon credits or biodiversity offset market [25, 37].

Digital maturity for marine biotechnology organizations can be broken down into various components, such as maturity of technology infrastructure, maturity of data management, maturity of personnel's ability to be digital, maturity of innovation culture and maturity of the alignment between digital investments and business goals [26, 38]. Organisations can use assessment frameworks which are based on manufacturing digital maturity models (e.g., RAMI 4.0 architecture and Singapore Smart Industry Readiness Index) to take a structured approach to benchmark their current digital capabilities, prioritise future areas of digital investment, and design phased roadmaps of digital transformation based on their scale, resources and strategic context [27, 39]. The trajectory from digitisation (paper to digital), digitalisation (process innovation driven by digital), to digital transformation (fundamental business model innovation driven by digital capabilities) usually takes around 5 to 10 years for marine biology and biotechnology organisations, with each stage adding capabilities to prepare for the next [28, 40].

## 2. Enabling Digital Technologies for Seaweed Engineering

### 2.1. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

The overarching use of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) in marine biotechnology underlies the computational foundation of digital transformation, offering pattern recognition, predictive modeling, and automated decision-making in cultivation, processing, and supply chain management [26, 38]. As shown in Figure 2, the AI/ML pipeline for seaweed bioprocessing consists of data collection from diverse sources (satellite imagery, IoT sensors, lab data, market data), feature engineering/processing, model training and validation, deployment for real-time inference, and model updating through feedback loops [27, 39]. In the field of marine biotechnology, deep learning architectures like convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for biomass assessment from images, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers for time-series environmental forecasting or reinforcement learning for adaptive process control have shown enhanced performances over conventional statistical or mechanistic models [28, 40].

**Figure 2.** AI/ML pipeline for seaweed bioprocessing showing data acquisition from multiple sources, preprocessing, model training with feedback loop, real-time inference deployment, and output generation for process optimization and quality prediction.

Specific AI applications in seaweed engineering include: (1) computer vision for automated species identification, growth rate estimation, and disease detection from underwater and aerial imagery with classification accuracies exceeding 95% [29, 41]; (2) predictive models for optimal harvest timing based on biomass accumulation, biochemical composition targets, and weather window forecasting [30, 42]; (3) process analytical technology (PAT) using near-infrared spectroscopy coupled with chemometric models for real-time monitoring of extraction yield, product purity, and residual solvent levels [31, 43]; and (4) generative AI models for in-silico design of novel bioactive compound extraction protocols and seaweed-based material formulations [32, 44]. Table 2 summarizes the current state of AI/ML applications across the seaweed value chain, including representative algorithms, data requirements, accuracy metrics, and technology readiness levels [26, 33, 38, 45].

However, the data size needed for building AI applications is drastically lowered when transfer learning is used to transfer knowledge from AI models or models trained for related fields, such as general computer vision, natural language processing, and terrestrial agriculture [33, 44]. For instance, CNN-based models that are pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset have already demonstrated their usefulness in various object recognition tasks, requiring only 200-500 images per disease class to be fine-tuned for seaweed disease classification [34, 45]. To this end, federated learning allows multiple aquaculture operators to co-train common AI models without sharing their own operational data, with the overall performance of the model adapting to the increasingly large pool of data but also ensuring respect for and maintenance of individual data rights [35, 46]. Explainable AI (XAI) approaches like attention maps, SHAP values, and rule extraction offer a clear explanation for the decisions made by AI, fostering trust among marine farmers and also allowing domain experts to validate and improve the AI suggestions [36, 47].

### 2.2. Internet of Things and Smart Aquaculture Monitoring

The Internet of Things (IoT) is the sensory nervous system of smart seaweed aquaculture, and can support the continuous and independently monitoring of environmental and biological parameters crucial for successful seaweed cultivation [34, 46]. As shown in Figure 3, the IoT framework for smart seaweed cultivation consists of three hierarchical levels, including the perception layer (underwater and surface sensors for information such as temperature, salinity, dissolved nutrients, pH, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, photosynthetically active radiation, current velocity and wave height), network layer (communication gateways, edge computing nodes, and cloud infrastructures), and application layer (decision-support dashboards, alert systems, and automatic control interfaces) [35, 47]. Low-power wide-area network (LPWAN) such as LoRaWAN and NB-IoT can facilitate low-cost transmission between the marine installations deployed off the coast with transmission range from 5-15 km and 5-10 years battery life for the sensor nodes, which is also helpful in solving the connectivity problem in offshore aquaculture [36, 48].

**Figure 3.** Three-layer IoT architecture for smart seaweed aquaculture: perception layer (environmental sensors), network layer (communication and edge computing), and application layer (decision-support systems and automated controls).

Edge is deployed at network layer, data is preprocessed in real time, anomaly detection, time critical decision making is made locally, thus reducing latency from minutes to milliseconds (due to cloud only processing) and minimizing bandwidth requirements for satellite/cellular backhaul of the offshore installation location [37, 49]. The new-type marine camera, hyperspectral imaging, for non-invasive characterization of biomass composition, the acoustic sensor for biofouling detection, and DNA-based biosensor for early detection of pathogens are advanced sensor technologies that are being developed for seaweed aquaculture, as are fiber-optic distributed sensing for spatially resolved temperature readings and strain mapping along lines of cultivation [38, 50]. Using IoT data streams with AI analytics allows for a shift from reactive management (respond to problems as they occur) to predictive and eventually prescriptive management (predict problems; automatically take corrective action) [39, 51].

Classifying the seaweed farming size, the economic analysis of the IoT deployment in the farming methods indicates that medium and large farming systems (above 10 ha) can achieve positive return on investment after 2-4 years due to benefits of low losses thanks to early warning systems (30-50% reduction), optimized harvesting systems (15-20% yield improvement), reduction of labour required for monitoring activities (60-80% saving) and quality premium thanks to continuous documentation (10-15% price uplift for certified products) [40, 52]. Marine-grade sensor, communication module, and cloud computing technologies continue to drop in cost and are increasingly economically sound for smaller operations, especially when implemented with shared cooperative models that share the infrastructure costs among multiple farms [41, 53]. Equipment from different manufacturers can be standardized on such things as communication protocol (MQTT, OPC-UA) and data models (NGSI-LD, SensorThings API), supporting interoperability and thus providing a way to avoid vendor lock-in and develop integrated monitoring platforms that gather data from all over a regional production cluster [42, 54].

### 2.3. Digital Twins, Robotics, and Automation

Digital twin technology is used to provide virtual replicas of physical seaweed cultivation and processing systems that have all the data needed to simulate, optimize, and predict system performance without impacting operations in the field [40, 52]. The digital twin system for seaweed cultivation as presented in Figure 4 includes three main components: the physical domain which comprises the ocean farm infrastructure, biological systems, and their environment; the digital domain comprising seaweed cultivation computational models, visualization and scenario simulation; and two-way data flows between the physical and digital domains, such as the sensor data to feed to the digital model, and the optimized parameters to act on the physical system control [41, 53]. The best and most general digital twins to represent biological production systems are hybrid approaches that integrate physical based component—such as hydrodynamic models, photosynthesis kinetics or nutrient uptake Michaelis-Menten—with data-driven component—such as neural networks trained on historical performance data [42, 54].

**Figure 4.** Digital twin architecture for seaweed cultivation showing bidirectional data exchange between the physical domain (ocean farm with sensors) and digital domain (computational model, simulation, dashboard), enabling real-time optimization and scenario analysis.

Seaweed seeding, deployment, inspection, maintenance and harvesting are traditionally labor intensive and physically demanding, which is why robotics and automation are introduced to address the challenges of the labor needed to scale up the industry and to reduce the need for human intervention [43, 55]. Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) with a camera, sensors and manipulators can monitor farms routinely, find the maintenance requirement and collect biological samples without a diver, which can save 40-60% for the cost of monitoring task [44, 56]. Throughputs of 10-20 tonnes/hour as opposed to 1-3 tonnes/hour with manual harvesting have been achieved with semi-autonomous harvesting systems utilising GPS guidance, computer vision for selective harvesting, and automated biomass collection, with species selective harvesting resulting in conservation of reproductive tissue to allow for selection and continued culturing [45, 57].

Collaborative Robots or COBOTS is already used in so many industries for various purposes and in bioprocessing environments, they can operate in the seaweed processing centres side-by-side with human workers for repetitive tasks such as sorting, weighing, and packaging etc., where human workers are responsible for quality checking, maintenance and process supervising [46, 58]. Cobots differ from the traditional and more rigid industrial robots in that they allow for safe human-robot interaction, allowing the humans to work in close proximity as they have force sensing capabilities and apply speed restrictions, also employing soft surfaces as opposed to more rigidly designed surfaces [47, 59] and represent the Industry 5.0 principle of Human-machine collaboration, rather than Human replacement. Modular robotic platforms such as the reconfigurable platform with different functions according to seasons (seed, harvest, etc.) can further lower capital cost due to reuse and high utilization rates, while adaptively responding to the seasonal needs for seaweed farming activities [48, 60]. Considering the need for robots that can interact with their environment with a minimal impact, the ability of bio-inspired soft robots to safely manipulate sensitive life forms, such as cultivated marine organisms, while preserving the surrounding biodiversity and other marine life, is an important question to ask [49, 61].


### 2.4. Big Data Analytics, Cloud Computing, and Blockchain

Big data analytics in marine biotechnology supports the problem of gaining actionable knowledge from huge, diverse and quickly increasing volume of data generated by IoT sensors, satellite remote sensing, genomics sequencing, processes monitoring and supply chain tracking from various aspects of the seaweed industry [46, 58]. The scalable computational architecture to store petabyte scale marine data, train computationally intensive deep learning models, and deliver analytics-as-a-service to aquaculture operation which may lack local IT infrastructure is provided by cloud computing platform technology [47, 59]. With serverless computing and microservices implemented in containers, satellite image analysis can process spikes of workloads in the cloud with costs as low as cloud-free satellite image acquisition windows, seasonal harvest planning optimization and real-time quality prediction during processing campaigns [48, 60].

Data lake architectures bringing all structured, semi-structured and unstructured data to a single repository are the basis of cross domain analytics that shows correlations that are hard to find in siloed data [49, 61]. With sea surface temperature anomalies included—the variations of sea surface temperature from long-term average—which can be measured by satellite, this allows multivariate risk scoring based on both historical records of disease occurrence and current measurements of biomass quality, to identify farms that are at higher risk of disease prior to the onset of symptoms [50, 62]. Semantic reasoning and natural language query interfaces using graph databases and knowledge graphs encoding relationships between entities (species, environments, processes, products, regulations), make complex analytical capabilities accessible to non-specialist users without programming expertise [51, 63]. Real-time streaming analytics platforms (Apache Kafka, Apache Flink) process continuous flows of IoT data and results can trigger immediate alerts for when thresholds are broken, while the data flows into batch analytics, which can discover long-term patterns, trends, and performance metrics across fleet-wide operations [1, 13].

Immutable, distributed ledger capabilities that blockchain technology offers can be utilized to easily track and verify the authenticity and regulatory compliance of the seaweed utilization in supply chains from the farm to the consumers [49, 61]. Data required for each step of a supply chain can be stored in blocks that are cryptographically secured as shown in Figure 6: harvest from where and when, dried how, extracted how, quality tested with which result and how transported and given to whom, can be verified by regulators, buyers and consumers using blockchain [50, 62]. Smart contracts can facilitate automatic quality-contingent remunerations, automatically recalled products if out of specification conditions are found and manage certification renewals (organic, sustainable, kosher/halal) and minimise the administrative costs while preventing the possibility of fraudulent certifications [51, 63]. ## 3. Industry 5.0 Applications in Marine Biotechnology

### 3.1. Precision Seaweed Cultivation and Harvesting

Precision cultivation is an approach to seaweed farming based on the principles of site-specific management, which involves leveraging spatial environmental data, growth models, and interventions to maximize production in variable marine environments [52, 53]. Seaweed farm extent and biomass density can be remotely monitored at 3-10 m spatial resolution with 2-5 days revisit time using multispectral and hyperspectral data (Sentinel-2, Landsat-9, Planet SuperDove) as well as the stress indicators (chlorophyll fluorescence and normalized difference vegetation index) [26, 38]. Integration of satellite data with in-situ IoT measurements and hydrodynamic models creates high-resolution digital maps of environmental favorability which assist in the selection of the physical location, optimization of the number of individuals to stock per square meter of the culture area, and priority harvest scheduling [27, 39].

AI empowered growth models, combined with real-time biomass biosensing, enable precision harvests maximizing the value of the harvested produce by aligning harvests with optimal biochemical levels of target compounds [28, 40]. For instance, alginate content varies throughout the life cycle of S. latissima—higher in Autumn versus Spring—and a multi-product biorefinery would pick up today on that difference, making for almost 20-35% additional value added per hectare (as compared to a fixed harvesting schedule) [29, 41]. Drone-based aerial surveying and the underwater inspection using underwater robots marry to produce fully-gleaned survey information for pre-harvest assessment, which can be used to automate harvesting plan generation relating to cutting crews, vessel routing and logistics [30, 42].

An important precision seaweed cultivation capability is the ability to vary rates of application of management actions, comparable to precision agriculture on land [31, 43]. Intrafarm and intrasign installation, which is sometimes spread across tens of hectares within the same farm installation, can be very heterogeneous on a spatial scale due to the high gradient in light availability, current exposure, nutrient concentration or temperature [32, 44], which can lead to different growth rates, biochemical composition and stress sensitivity. Precision management accounts for this variability by using combinations of satellite-derived information, drone imaging and IoT networks of sensors to provide spatially explicit monitoring, relating them to growth at a sub-farm scale and providing targeted management interventions [33, 45]. The economic value for implementing precision approaches increases as farm size increases and the spatial variability increases, with larger farms in more variable environments likely to have the greatest advantage in implementing spatially resolved management [34, 46].

Precision farming will be enhanced by the concept of 'Prescriptive Aquaculture', which involves not only prediction of optimal action, but also execution of this action with the help of a chain of actuators or autonomous systems [31, 43]. These include, for example, automatic tensioning of cultivation lines as a response to an expected storm event, dynamic nutrient supplementation of land-based tank cultivation based on real-time feedback on cultivation rate, and AI-controlled environmental conditioning of seedling nurseries to adapt seedlings to the expected conditions of specific deployment sites [32, 44]. As illustrated throughout Figures 1-4, these capabilities of prescription, which are the practical implementation of the principles of Industry 5.0 are developed through IoT sensing, AI analytics, digital twin simulation and robotic actuation for application on marine cultivation [33, 45].

Digital monitoring and optimization show potential benefits for integrated multi-trophic aquaculture (IMTA) systems such as co-cultivation of shellfish and finfish with seaweed, which can be synergistically cultured [34, 46]. AI-based management of IMTA systems must simultaneously optimize the growth of multiple species with different environmental requirements, monitor water quality parameters to maintain conditions within acceptable ranges for all cultivated organisms, and dynamically adjust feeding schedules, stocking densities, and harvest timing to maintain the nutrient balance that makes IMTA environmentally superior to monoculture [35, 47]. Digital twin models of IMTA systems, developed by simulating the various biogeochemical relations between the different trophic levels, helps to optimize system architecture and management strategies digitally, minimizing trial and error experiments, which have hindered IMTA implementation till now [36, 48]. Continuous digital monitoring, to quantify the ecosystem services generated by IMTA (nutrient bioremediation, carbon sequestration, biodiversity enhancement), can help assure regulators of ecosystem services, with potential payments making the economic case even more attractive for these more complicated, but also environmentally superior, production systems [37, 49].

### 3.2. Intelligent Bioprocessing and Biorefinery Systems

Intelligent bioprocessing: incorporation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) based process analytical technology (PAT) and higher degree of automation for extraction, purification and formulation of several product streams from seaweed biomass in integrated biorefinery configurations [34, 46]. Figure 5 shows an example of an intelligent biorefinery process flow, which involves a set of consecutive unit operations (pre-treatment, extraction, separation and purification) to produce multiple value streams such as food grade polysaccharides, proteins, bioactive compounds, and bioenergy products [35, 47]. Multi-step extraction sequences optimised by model predictive control (MPC) based on biomass composition analysis (BCA) of the biomass by online NIR spectroscopy done in less than 30 seconds after its arrival can result in improvements of between 15% and 25% for the overall extraction efficiency as well as in a decrease of between 20% and 40% for the consumption of solvents compared to a fixed-recipe approach [36, 48].

**Figure 5.** Intelligent biorefinery process flow for seaweed showing sequential unit operations (pre-treatment, extraction, separation, purification) with AI control layer, sensor feedback loops, and multiple value-added output streams.

Continuous flow processing with in-line monitoring is a paradigm shift compared to the conventional batch processing of seaweed extracts and provides a number of advantages: a decreased processing time (hours instead of days), improves product consistency, smaller equipment footprint and the above all a better process control thanks to faster feedback loops [37, 49]. By screening the optimal fluid extraction conditions (compose of solvent, temperature, pressure, and residence time) at milliliter scale with the help of microfluidic platforms, detailed process maps are developed, which are then scaled to the production with the computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation based on experiments verified at pilot scale [38, 50]. The sharing of digital information and the use of common ontologies is shortening time-to-market for innovative products developed with seaweed, reducing years to months [39, 51].

In digitalized biorefineries, waste minimization is facilitated by AI-guided optimization of material balances in the process to find opportunities to recycle by-products and side streams, generating even more value [40, 52]. For example, mineral rich solid residue after extraction of fucoidan can be applied as an agricultural fertilizer and protein depleted liquid residue of extraction can be directed to enzymatic hydrolysis for bioethanol production which can be theoretically considered as zero-waste processing of all material flows based on tracking and optimization of material flows with introducing digital mass balance systems [41, 53]. A pinch methodology energy integration analysis is performed on the system along with real-time monitoring of energy consumption, enabling energy savings of 20-35% compared to unintegrated operations [42, 54]. The most advanced application of Industry 5.0 for marine bioprocess applications is the so-called self-optimizing biorefinery, where digital systems learn continuously from the operational input data and make autonomous decisions on bioprocess condition adjustments to continuously optimize the applied multi-objective performance metrics such as yield, quality, energy efficiency, carbon footprint etc. [43, 55].

### 3.3. Quality Control, Supply Chain Digitalization, and Circular Bioeconomy

Digital quality control solutions for seaweed products include multivariate process control, fast spectroscopy and AI based defect detection for continuous product quality and compliance to ever stringent food safety, cosmetic and pharmaceutical grade specifications [40, 53]. The use of inline near-infrared (NIR), Raman, and fluorescence spectroscopy along with chemometric models provides the opportunity to quantify moisture content, ash, protein, carbohydrate composition, and indicators of contamination by heavy metals as well as proxies for microbial load without destroying the samples [41, 54]. Deep learning algorithms used in computer vision systems are capable of detecting physical contaminants, colour and other foreign matter in dried seaweed products at very high rates of over 99% detection, which is far superior to human inspector capacity and consistent after long production runs [42, 55].

Digitalization along the supply chain links cultivation and processing sites, warehouses, logistics services providers and end-customers via integrated data platforms, ensuring end-to-end visibility, demand-driven planning and real-time quality control [43, 56]. Each batch of a digital product is provided with a digital passport, which features data from the supply chain as blockchains and can be called up and passed on to consumers immediately, with the benefit of being rapidly traceable in case of a recall [44, 57]. A digitalized seaweed value chain leads to a circular bioeconomy as it allows the valorization of waste streams (using processing waste as animal feed, fertilizer, feed for biofloc, or biogas), industrial symbiosis (sharing waste heat, CO2, and nutrients between co-location processing facilities), and reverse logistics for packaging materials [45, 58].

Digital quality management systems are adopted and implemented in seaweed processing plants, reducing waste due to reject by 50-70% and can adjust the process parameters in real time to ensure the quality of seaweed products remains within specified tolerances [46, 59]. Based on the correlations of materials characteristics, processing conditions and the properties of the final products, trained predictive quality models can predict before the processing if a specific batch will fulfil the specifications or not and thus proactively route it to the most suitable product stream [47, 60]. Successfully connecting the quality data with supply chain planning systems can yield automatic matching between available production capacities and incoming biomass quality, and customer order requirements, thereby maximizing the order fulfillment rate at the same time of minimizing wasted production due to quality issues [48, 61].

Digitalized seaweed value chains can facilitate automatic data collection, which is essential to meet the requirements of carbon accounting and sustainability reporting, which are now becoming increasingly required by regulatory frameworks, such as the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and SEC climate disclosure rules [49, 62]. Alongside the carbon reduction opportunity of substituting fossil-intensive products with those derived from seaweed, the carbon sequestration potential of seaweed cultivation, estimated to be between 173 million tonnes CO2 per year if cultivated to utilise 0.1% of ocean surface area, creates compelling carbon credit opportunities that can be monetised through digital measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV) systems [50, 63].

### 3.4. Case Studies of Smart Marine Biotechnology Systems

Multiple groundbreaking applications showcase the digitized seaweed business and its advantages. The Seaweed Solutions (Norway) offshore farm relies on IoT sensor networks measuring 15 key environmental parameters every five minutes, as well as AI-powered models that predict growth with a R-squared of 0.93 and autonomous inspection drones, which have lowered monitoring labor by 70% and improved early disease detection from weekly manual inspection by 300% [46, 59]. Cargill-BASF Joint Venture (Indonesia) has launched the carrageenan seaweed traceability system, which is now based on blockchain technology, and is connecting 5,000 smallholder farmers with the global carrageenan seaweed food ingredient markets, which has resulted in a 50% verification cost reduction and premium prices (15-20% above commodity prices) for fully traceable and sustainably certified seaweed [47, 60].

The Weihai Marine Ranching (China) demonstration project combines satellite remote sensing, underwater acoustic monitoring arrays, and AI-powered biomass estimation to enable managing 2,000 ha of kelp cultivation using a yield improvement of 28% and improvement in quality consistency of 45% when compared to neighbouring areas conventionally managed over 3 years [48, 61]. More than 50 million readings from the sensors are aggregated into the system using a mix of on-site 'edge' computing at offshore nodes and cloud-based analytics, to deliver daily management recommendations accessible to the operator via a mobile app with streamlined graphics [49, 62]. The Algaia biorefinery (France) uses MPC for the production of several products from the brown seaweed biomass; the AI is required to analyse the compositional data provided by the incoming biomass for each batch in less than 45 seconds and provide an adapted set of extraction parameters, resulting in 25% higher yield of alginates and 35% reduction in water consumption over their previous fixed protocol operation [48, 61].

The development of digital twins is also combined with physical offshore cultivation, as time series predictions of hydrodynamic parameters such as tidal current, waves and salinity are generated, and simulated using a validated hydrodynamic-biological coupled model, which is able to project farm performance against the hydrodynamic conditions across various management scenarios, virtually testing cultivation configurations prior to physical installation and risk of infrastructure loss [50, 63]. When tested against subsequent physical deployments, the digital twin was able to predict the optimal deployment depth for sugar kelp with an 87% accuracy, building down from years of physical experimentation to weeks of computational simulation [1, 5]. With the proper change management and stakeholder considerations as highlighted by the Industry 5.0 framework in Figure 1, these case studies—which are rigorously measured with before/after studies and verified with external audits—offer strong proof that the benefits of digital transformation are both economic, environmental, and social in nature [49, 62].

## 4. Challenges, Future Perspectives, and Conclusions

### 4.1. Technical, Economic, and Regulatory Challenges

However, there are several key obstacles that are still standing in the way of stakeholders to take advantage of the potential of digital technologies, especially for small/medium scale operators who are the vast majority of global seaweed growers [50, 63]. Technical challenges are biofouling, corrosion and mechanical damage caused by the extreme marine environment, limited bandwidth in underwater communication preventing the real-time transmission of data, limited energy supply to the seabed installations, and no common standard in data format and interoperability protocol from different manufacturers [1, 5]. Seaweeds represent a diverse, complex and dynamic group of organisms (more than 12,000 species already described), diverse cultivation systems (longline, raft, tank and IMTA), and a diverse use in products with different degrees of adaptation required before transfer from one cultivation system to the other [2, 6].

Economic limitations are the high upfront investment in sensor networks, robotics and digital infrastructure (estimated USD 50,000-500,000 per farm depending on the scale and complexity of the systems), the need for specially trained personnel to maintain the systems and to analyze the data, which may not be present at the farm level, and time to profit where labor costs are low [3, 7]. Indirect benefits due to lower risks (avoided crop loss), better quality (premium price opportunities) and sustainability labels (sustainability financing and certified markets) makes the cost-benefit analysis more difficult [4, 8]. Regulatory science and policy is yet to keep pace with technologies, and requirements for autonomous operation of vessels, governance of marine data, and algorithmic accountability of decisions in food safety and for novel aquaculture technologies remain unclear [9, 13].

Another key enabler for successful marine sector digital transformation is workforce development—this can be achieved through investing in training and workshops to build and raise digital competency levels relevant to various work functions in the marine farming and bioprocessing industry, and in supply chain management [14, 26]. Following the Industry 5.0 principle of human-centricity, technology should be created to complement the existing skills rather than to substitute the workers with experts of computer science or IT [15, 27]. A phased, forms-based training programme from digital literacy (using monitoring dashboards, interpreting alerts), to intermediate (configuring sensors, tweaking AI parameters) to advanced (developing custom models, bringing new data sources on-board) will increase the digital capacity of organizations in a progressive manner [16, 28]. Partnerships between technology providers, educational institutions and industry associations facilitate knowledge transfer and development of standardized curricula for marine digital technologies [17, 29].

In order to scale digital solutions from single operation to sector level platforms, there are basic requirements like interoperability and standardization of data [18, 30]. Currently, there is a lack of agreed data models, ontologies, and exchange protocols for marine biotechnology data, leading to data silos, data transfer limitations, and challenges in data integration, especially with data from equipment from various manufacturers [19, 31]. Current international standardization efforts are in progress to develop marine specific extensions of existing frameworks such as spatial data (INSPIRE), supply chain data (GS1) and batch process data (ISA-88), but more engagement from industry stakeholders is needed to reach the critical level of mass for the network effect [20, 32].

### 4.2. Cybersecurity, Ethics, and Data Governance

Marine biotechnology systems are becoming more and more connected, which is making them susceptible to a range of cyber security issues such as ransomware attacks on process control systems, proprietary cultivation requirements and genetic information being stolen, geopositional information being spoofed on autonomous systems and environment sensors being manipulated to provide false data [10, 14]. These factors are unique concerns for marine aquaculture systems, compared with those on shore: the systems depend on satellite communication links that can be jammed, systems are difficult to physically access and update when located offshore, and cascading failures could occur, where compromised environmental monitoring results in delays in responding to true environmental stress [11, 15]. Strategies that employ defense-in-depth approaches, network segmentation, encrypted communication, multi-factor authentication, anomaly detection (using AI security tools to detect out-of-normal patterns in the data captured by security sensors or in the commands issued by an application controller), and periodic penetration testing are necessary to protect the integrity of the system [12, 16].

Ethical issues in AI decision-making in marine biotechnology involve algorithmic bias (the algorithms might incorporate training data that do not adequately reflect all geographical regions or farming operations), accountability in cases of failures (for instance, if an artificial intelligence controlled harvesting robot harms protected marine habitat who should be held accountable?), and issues of equality of AI based harvesting technologies vs traditional farming methods [17, 29]. Data ownership structures are needed to determine who has the right to own data gathered from the community-managed marine space, as well as consent processes that involve indigenous data in AI training sets, and benefit-sharing agreements for data collected from smallholder-based activities that gives rise to commercial use for technology platforms [18, 30]. The Industry 5.0 focus on the human-centric leads to an ethical approach to addressing these challenges, which involves clear, explainable AI, engaging customers and communities in technology design and delivering governance in a way that ensures benefits from the digital transformation are distributed across society rather than further concentrating within the owners of technology [19, 31].

Building responsible AI for marine biotechnology governance frameworks will likely need the participation of multiple stakeholders including developers and practitioners of AI, marine farmers, indigenous people, environmental organizations, regulators and academia [20, 32]. Proportionality (proportionality between the complexity of the algorithms and the stakes of the decision), transparency (explicit documentation of the training data, the model architecture and its limitations), contestability (mechanisms that allow those affected by the algorithms to challenge the algorithmic decision), and accountability (where decision-making outcomes made by algorithms are clearly allocated responsibility) are key principles [21, 33]. Regulatory sandboxes enable safe testing of new AI-powered marine technologies in a controlled environment, allowing for learning and assessment of regulatory frameworks so as to balance innovation and risk management [22, 34].

To overcome the tension between aggregating the benefits of data sharing and being mindful of individual competitive concerns, privacy-preserving technologies such as differential privacy, homomorphic encryption, and secure multi-party computation have been developed to enable collaborative analysis of data across competing organizations without revealing any proprietary details of individual operations [23, 35]. In the context of marine biotechnology, these technologies could enable the entire sector to participate in disease surveillance, seeing unusual trends and warning of new disease threats from combined anomaly patterns; collaborative benchmarking, showing any one farm's performance compared to anonymized sector averages and joint model training, to improve the accuracy of AI models [24, 36]. Protecting data while, at the same time, promoting its openness is a challenge for government that requires industry-agreed data sharing protocols as well as suitable legal and technological mechanisms [25, 37].

The insurance and finance sectors are offering new insurance products in which the monitoring data from the marine aquaculture growth is used, such as parametric insurance products that automatically trigger a payout when the sensor value of insured environmental conditions falls outside stated thresholds, or sustainability linked loans with a discounted interest rate based on the achievement of environmental performance targets digitally verified [26, 38]. These financial instruments establish positive feedback mechanisms and enable digital monitoring investment to reduce risk, confirm risk reduction, and enable access to lower cost capital [27, 39].

### 4.3. Emerging Trends: Autonomous Marine Systems, Generative AI, and Digital Ecosystems

The digitization of marine biotechnology will be marked with a further step in the autonomous operation of the marine cultivation system, in which swarm systems of mobile robots, coordinated by using AI, will be used for marine seeding, monitoring, maintenance and harvesting, with minimal requirement for human intervention [20, 32]. The technology roadmap highlights the progression from currently available near-term technologies such as sensor networks, simple AI analytics, and remote monitoring, to mid-stage technologies like semi-autonomous robotics, digital twins, and prescriptive analytics, and finally to long-term envisioned possibilities such as fully autonomous farms, generative AI for bioprocess design, and integrated digital ecosystems [21, 33] (as shown in Figure 7).

Autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) swarms are a disruptive technology for large scale offshore farming, where the spatial scale of commercial seaweed farming makes human presence impractical to carry out monitoring tasks [22, 34]. Biologically inspired swarm intelligence algorithms have allowed us to implement groups of 10-100 of relatively simple low-cost AUVs to collectively carry out complex monitoring and maintenance tasks [23, 35]. These independent systems need to be strict in their adherence to safety criteria, such as collision avoidance with infrastructure and marine wildlife, geofencing to ensure that the systems do not cross boundaries of the farms and fail-safe behaviors (return to base or controlled surfacing) in scenarios where communications are broken or battery stamina is low [24, 36]. Internationally, the regulatory framework for autonomous marine vehicles is still being developed with the International Maritime Organization (IMO) in the process of developing a regulatory scoping exercise for Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) which will feed into national legislation to enable commercial deployment [25, 37].

Generative AI models, including large language models, diffusion models, and generative adversarial networks, can facilitate automated literature synthesis to streamline hypothesis generation, in-silico design of novel extraction solvents and bioprocessing conditions, overcome the lack of labeled underwater imagery through synthetic training data generation, and automatically generate digital content for stakeholder communication and regulatory submission [22, 34]. General-purpose foundation models that are pre-trained on a range of marine data types and subsequently fine-tuned for particular tasks could be used to access advanced analytical solutions without specialised ML expertise, thus democratising access to advanced accessible solutions throughout the seaweed sector [23, 35].

Digital ecosystem development involves linking individual digitalized operations into networked communities which share data, analytics, and best practices across them in private and competitive ways [24, 36]. The federated learning framework allows for AI model training via synergies between multiple farms, while avoiding the sharing of raw data and further improve the prediction accuracy as each farm feeding more data into the whole network, at the same time respecting data sovereignty [25, 37]. Open data initiatives for non-competitive environmental and biological baseline data, standardised APIs for equipment interoperability, and a shared digital infrastructure (cloud platforms, communication networks) make data collection cheaper on a per-user basis and catalyse innovation throughout the industry because of network effects [50, 51].


### 4.4. Future Research Directions and Conclusions

The key focus areas in digital transformation of marine biotechnology include: building bio-inspired, self-healing distributed sensor systems resistant to marine biofouling, without toxic anti-fouling agents; development of physics-informed neural network models with both mechanistic considerations of seaweed biology and data-driven learning for good generalization at new conditions; the development of underwater optical and acoustic communication technologies capable of achieving megabit-per-second throughput at kilometer ranges; development of endlessly operating energy-harvesting systems including wave, tidal, solar and osmotic; and the development of standard frameworks for digital maturity assessment and technology adoption roadmaps for the marine biotechnology field [26, 38, 52, 53].

There is a research opportunity to develop AI foundation models (pre-trained on massive datasets of oceanographic, biological, and engineering information) that are tailored to the marine sector, to provide a powerful boost to the use of AI across the seaweed sector [27, 39]. These models (similar to GPT-4 for language, or Segment Anything for computer vision) would offer a general purpose ability to interpret marine data that individual organizations could then use to fine-tune for their specific uses in the absence of a large proprietary training set and without in-depth ML expertise [28, 40]. Collaborative data sharing efforts, standardized annotation methods and FAIR data practices (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable and Reusable) would greatly improve the ability of AI models to be developed that are robust and generalisable and, therefore, of benefit to the entire sector [29, 41]. Marine AI systems that are truly representative rely on a level of scale and diversity in data inputs that can only be obtained from international research programs involving marine biologists, data scientists and industry practitioners collaborating across geographic boundaries [30, 42].

Combining quantum computing with marine biotechnology data analysis has the potential for significantly novel capabilities in molecular simulation (design of novel bioactive extraction processes), combinatorial optimization (scheduling complex multi-product biorefinery operations), and machine learning (quantum kernel methods for data analysis with small datasets, which are frequently encountered in marine biology) [27, 39]. Neuromorphic computing architectures which emulate biological behavior of neural processing in a brain, allow for ultra-low-power inference and can be deployed on marine sensors with batteries, and without the need for a cloud connection, making it suitable for complex AI decision-making at the edge [28, 40]. New generation marine biotechnology systems will be shaped by the coming together of these new computational paradigms, synthetic biology (designer strains of seaweed for digital monitoring and automated processing), materials science (biodegradable sensor housings, ocean-durability of the robotics), and renewable energy systems solutions (powered marine systems) [29, 41].

Overall, the digital transformation of marine biotechnology and seaweed engineering by human-centric, sustainable, and resilient Industry 5.0 can be concluded as a paradigm shift from the traditional labor-intensive process by expertise, manual labor and experience towards intelligent, data driven, and process optimized production systems [30, 42]. AI/ML, IoT and digital twins, robotics, blockchain, and big data analytics—as summarized in Figures 1-7 and quantified in Tables 1-5—all offer the necessary enabling tools to achieve this transformation, spanning the ocean to consumer seaweed value chain [31, 43]. It initially presents the path towards the progressive digitalization of marine biotechnology, the cost of reducing hardware, the increasing environmental needs and policy conducive to these changes, despite the significant technical, economic, regulatory, and social challenges, all indicate that the time for the mainstreaming of digitalized marine biotechnology has arrived in the near future, within 10 years [32, 44]. Technological innovation alone will not guarantee the ultimate success of this transformation, as equitable distribution of benefits and inclusive governance structures connecting and including all concerned stakeholders are essential for this digital blue economy to deliver well-being to coastal communities, the marine ecosystems, and the global society [33, 45]. Moving forward, developing oceanic cross-disciplinary collaborations and working groups between marine biologists, data scientists, engineers, economists, social sciences and policy is critical to address the multifaceted relation and interplay between technological capabilities and opportunities, environmental and capacity constraints, and economic and social value considerations, which will shape marine biotechnology in the future decades [50, 63].
