# Artificial Intelligence for Inclusive Growth and Social Equity: Bridging the Gap in the Fourth Industrial Revolution

---

## Section 1: The Foundation — Understanding the AI Equity Gap

### 1.1 Defining Inclusive Growth and Social Equity in the AI Era

The Fourth Industrial Revolution has ushered in a period of unprecedented technological transformation, with artificial intelligence standing at its vanguard [1]. Yet, as societies grapple with the implications of machine learning algorithms, autonomous decision-making systems, and predictive analytics, a fundamental question persists: who benefits from this revolution, and who is left behind [2]? Answering this question requires a thorough re-examination of what we mean by growth, progress, and equity in an age where intelligent machines increasingly shape the contours of economic and social life [3].


Inclusive growth, in its most essential formulation, refers to economic expansion that creates opportunities for all segments of a population while distributing the resulting benefits fairly across society [4]. Traditional metrics of economic progress—gross domestic product, per capita income, aggregate productivity—have long been recognized as insufficient barometers of genuine human welfare [5]. A nation may post impressive growth figures while vast portions of its citizenry remain trapped in poverty, lacking access to education, healthcare, or meaningful employment [6]. The AI era intensifies this paradox. Technology companies may generate extraordinary wealth and efficiency gains through intelligent automation, yet these gains can concentrate among a narrow stratum of shareholders, engineers, and early adopters unless deliberate policy and institutional design intervene [7].

Social equity, as a concept, extends beyond the mere distribution of material resources [8]. It encompasses the fairness of processes, the recognition of diverse identities and needs, and the active dismantling of structural barriers that prevent marginalized communities from participating fully in civic and economic life [9]. In the context of AI, social equity demands that we interrogate not only who receives the outputs of intelligent systems but also who participates in their design, who controls the data that feeds them, and whose values are encoded in their decision-making architectures [10]. Social equity is fundamentally a mutually constructed phenomenon—it concerns not merely what individuals receive from society but also what they contribute to it, recognizing that genuine fairness requires reciprocal obligations between citizens and institutions [11].

The Sustainable Development Goals adopted by the United Nations provide a crucial normative framework for understanding inclusive growth in the AI era [12]. SDG 10, which calls for the reduction of inequalities within and among countries, speaks directly to the distribution question that AI raises [13]. The overarching pledge embedded in the 2030 Agenda to "leave no one behind" serves as both an ethical imperative and a practical benchmark against which AI deployment strategies must be measured [14]. When an algorithm determines creditworthiness, allocates educational resources, or triages medical patients, its design and training must reflect this commitment to universality and non-discrimination [15].

The intersection of AI and inclusive growth further demands attention to procedural justice—the fairness of the mechanisms through which decisions are made and resources allocated [16]. An AI system that produces equitable outcomes through opaque processes still falls short of genuine social equity because it denies affected populations the ability to understand, contest, or shape the decisions that govern their lives [17]. Transparency, accountability, and participatory governance are therefore not ancillary concerns but central pillars of any framework that seeks to harness AI for truly inclusive development [18].


### 1.2 The Rise of the AI Equity Gap

For much of the early twenty-first century, discourse around technological inequality centered on the "digital divide"—the gap between those with access to internet connectivity and computing devices and those without [19]. While this divide remains consequential, particularly in rural and low-income communities across the Global South, the emergence of sophisticated AI systems has introduced a more complex and multidimensional form of inequality [20]. The AI Equity Gap represents the distance between what artificial intelligence is capable of achieving and the extent to which its benefits reach all members of society equitably [21]. Unlike the binary framing of the digital divide—connected or unconnected—the AI Equity Gap operates along a spectrum, encompassing disparities in data representation, algorithmic fairness, computing infrastructure, human capital, and institutional readiness [22].

The concentration of AI capabilities in a small number of nations and corporations constitutes one of the most striking features of this emerging divide. Research output in AI, measured by published papers, patents, and deployed systems, remains overwhelmingly dominated by the United States, China, and a handful of European and East Asian countries [23]. The computational infrastructure required to train large-scale models—specialized hardware, massive data centers, and the energy to power them—is similarly concentrated [24]. A mere handful of technology companies command the financial and technical resources necessary to develop frontier AI systems, creating what some scholars have termed a "compute oligopoly" that determines the direction of technological progress according to commercial imperatives rather than public need [25][26].

This concentration extends to talent. The researchers, engineers, and data scientists who design and build AI systems are disproportionately located in wealthy nations, often recruited from developing countries through compensation packages that local institutions cannot match [27]. This dynamic produces a persistent brain drain that impoverishes the very nations most in need of technical expertise to adapt AI to local challenges [28]. Universities in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America struggle to retain faculty with AI expertise, undermining their capacity to train the next generation of local innovators [29].

The data dimension of the AI Equity Gap deserves particular attention. AI systems are only as representative as the data on which they are trained [30]. When training datasets overrepresent certain populations, languages, or contexts while neglecting others, the resulting models perform poorly—or actively harm—underrepresented groups [31]. Natural language processing systems trained predominantly on English-language text struggle to serve speakers of indigenous or low-resource languages [32]. Medical diagnostic algorithms trained on data from affluent populations may misidentify conditions in patients from different ethnic or socioeconomic backgrounds [33]. The principle that AI is not neutral but rather absorbs and amplifies weaknesses in existing systems becomes starkly apparent when examining these data-driven disparities [34].

The AI Equity Gap is further reinforced by institutional factors. Nations without robust regulatory frameworks, ethical oversight mechanisms, or strategic AI policies find themselves in a reactive posture, adopting technologies designed elsewhere without the capacity to shape them according to local values and priorities [35]. This institutional deficit creates a dependency relationship in which developing countries become consumers of AI products designed for other contexts rather than creators of solutions tailored to their own challenges [36]. The resulting dynamic risks reproducing colonial patterns of technological dependence in digital form, with knowledge and power flowing from periphery to center rather than circulating equitably [37].


### 1.3 The "AI Sustainability Exclusion Theory"

Understanding why certain populations are systematically excluded from the developmental benefits of artificial intelligence requires a theoretical framework that moves beyond surface-level explanations of access and affordability [38]. The AI Sustainability Exclusion Theory provides such a framework by identifying interconnected biases and structural barriers that interact to produce persistent patterns of exclusion [39]. This theory argues that the mere deployment of AI technology in a given context is insufficient to generate inclusive outcomes; what matters are the underlying conditions that enable safe, trusted, and sustained use over time [40].

The theory identifies several mutually reinforcing mechanisms of exclusion. First, infrastructural exclusion operates when populations lack the physical prerequisites for AI engagement—reliable electricity, internet connectivity, appropriate computing devices, and the telecommunications backbone necessary for data transmission [41]. Without these material foundations, even the most brilliantly designed AI application remains inaccessible. Second, data exclusion occurs when communities are absent from the datasets that train AI systems, rendering them invisible to algorithms that increasingly mediate access to services, opportunities, and resources [42]. Communities that do not generate digital data traces—whether due to informal economies, oral cultural traditions, or deliberate resistance to surveillance—find themselves excluded from AI-driven systems by default [43].

Third, capability exclusion arises when populations lack the skills, knowledge, and institutional support necessary to engage meaningfully with AI systems—not merely as passive consumers but as informed users capable of understanding, questioning, and shaping these technologies [44]. This form of exclusion encompasses both individual human capital (digital literacy, critical thinking, technical skills) and collective institutional capacity (regulatory expertise, research infrastructure, educational systems) [45]. Fourth, governance exclusion manifests when communities and nations are absent from the decision-making processes that shape AI's trajectory—from international standards bodies and industry consortia to national policy deliberations and corporate boardrooms [46].

The AI Sustainability Exclusion Theory emphasizes that these mechanisms do not operate in isolation but rather compound one another in self-reinforcing cycles [47]. A community lacking infrastructure cannot generate data, cannot develop skills, and cannot participate in governance—each deficit amplifying the others. Breaking these cycles requires coordinated interventions across all four dimensions simultaneously, rather than piecemeal approaches that address one form of exclusion while leaving others intact [48].

Critically, the theory shifts analytical attention from the question of "what AI can do" to the practical conditions that determine whether AI delivers sustained improvements in people's lives. A pilot project demonstrating AI's potential in a controlled setting means little if the surrounding ecosystem cannot support long-term deployment, maintenance, adaptation, and governance [49]. Sustainability in this context refers not to environmental concerns alone but to the durability and resilience of AI's beneficial impacts over time—the capacity for communities to continue deriving value from intelligent systems even as technology evolves, funding fluctuates, and political priorities shift [50].

## Section 2: The Infrastructure and Enablers of Inclusive AI

### 2.1 The Three Levers: Infrastructure, Data, and Skills

Translating the aspiration of inclusive AI into tangible reality requires deliberate investment in three interdependent leverage points: physical and digital infrastructure, representative and accessible data ecosystems, and broadly distributed human skills [51]. These three levers, when activated in concert, create the enabling environment within which AI can serve diverse populations rather than privileged minorities alone. Neglecting any single lever undermines the effectiveness of the other two, producing fragile systems incapable of delivering equitable outcomes at scale.

The infrastructure dimension encompasses far more than internet connectivity, though connectivity remains a necessary precondition. Reliable electricity supply constitutes the most fundamental requirement—without consistent power, computing devices cannot function, data centers cannot operate, and digital services cannot reach end users [52]. Across much of Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Latin America, intermittent electricity access remains a binding constraint that no amount of software innovation can overcome [53]. Beyond electrification, the infrastructure challenge extends to telecommunications networks capable of handling the data volumes that AI applications demand, last-mile connectivity that reaches rural and remote communities, and crucially, computing infrastructure—the servers, graphics processing units, and cloud platforms on which AI models are trained and deployed [54].

The computing dimension of infrastructure merits particular emphasis because it represents a newer and less widely recognized form of inequality. Training sophisticated AI models requires access to specialized hardware and enormous computational resources that remain prohibitively expensive for most institutions in developing countries [55]. Cloud computing offers a partial solution by allowing researchers and organizations to rent computational capacity rather than purchasing it outright, but this approach creates ongoing dependency on foreign providers and raises questions about data sovereignty when sensitive information must be transmitted to servers located in other jurisdictions [56]. Building domestic computing capacity—through national data centers, regional computing cooperatives, or shared research infrastructure—represents a strategic investment in long-term technological autonomy [57].

The data lever addresses the representativeness, quality, and accessibility of the information that feeds AI systems. Promoting open data initiatives and data-sharing arrangements can democratize access to the raw material of machine learning, enabling researchers and developers in resource-constrained settings to build systems tailored to local contexts [58]. However, open data alone is insufficient if the underlying datasets reflect the same biases and blind spots that characterize existing information systems [59]. Deliberate efforts to collect data from underrepresented communities, in local languages and reflecting local conditions, are essential to building AI systems that serve diverse populations accurately [60].

The skills lever operates at multiple levels simultaneously. At the foundation, basic digital literacy enables populations to interact with AI-powered services as informed users rather than passive recipients. At intermediate levels, data literacy and computational thinking empower workers across sectors to collaborate productively with intelligent systems [61]. At advanced levels, specialized expertise in machine learning, data science, and AI engineering enables local innovation and adaptation. Building this multi-layered skills ecosystem requires sustained investment in educational institutions. Partnerships between academia and industry can ensure that curricula remain current and that graduates possess practically relevant competencies [62]. Lifelong learning programs acknowledge that the rapid pace of technological change demands continuous skill renewal throughout working careers, not merely front-loaded education during youth [63].


### 2.2 Beyond Connectivity: The Role of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)

The concept of Digital Public Infrastructure has emerged as a powerful framework for understanding how foundational digital systems can serve as platforms for inclusive service delivery [64]. DPI encompasses the shared digital systems that underpin entire economies and societies—digital identity verification, electronic payment systems, data exchange protocols, and interoperable registries [65]. These systems function as digital analogs to physical infrastructure like roads, electrical grids, and water systems: they provide the common substrate upon which diverse applications and services can be built.

The integration of AI with existing Digital Public Infrastructure represents both an extraordinary opportunity and a significant governance challenge. When AI is layered onto identity systems, it can improve authentication accuracy, detect fraud, and streamline access to public services [66]. When combined with digital payment infrastructure, it can enable personalized financial products, predictive poverty mapping, and targeted social protection transfers [67]. When integrated with health information exchanges, it can support diagnostic assistance, epidemiological surveillance, and optimized resource allocation. Each of these applications holds genuine potential to improve lives at population scale.

However, the marriage of AI and DPI also introduces risks that demand careful governance. Digital identity systems augmented with biometric AI can become instruments of surveillance and social control if adequate safeguards are not established [68]. Payment systems enhanced with predictive algorithms can enable discriminatory profiling or unjust exclusion from financial services [69]. Health data platforms combined with machine learning can compromise patient privacy or produce systematically biased recommendations for certain demographic groups. The foundational nature of DPI means that errors or injustices introduced at this level propagate throughout the entire ecosystem of services built upon it.

Ensuring that AI-enhanced DPI serves as a platform for inclusion rather than a vector for exclusion requires several governance principles. Interoperability standards must prevent vendor lock-in and ensure that communities are not trapped within proprietary ecosystems that limit their choices [70]. Data minimization principles must constrain the collection and retention of personal information to what is strictly necessary for legitimate purposes. Transparency requirements must enable affected populations to understand how AI-driven decisions about their identity, finances, or health are made [71]. Accountability mechanisms must provide avenues for redress when systems malfunction or produce discriminatory outcomes.

The experience of countries that have deployed DPI at scale offers instructive lessons. India's Aadhaar digital identity system has enabled hundreds of millions of citizens to access government services and financial products for the first time, yet it has also generated persistent controversies around privacy, exclusion errors, and surveillance potential [72]. Estonia's X-Road data exchange platform demonstrates how interoperability can enable efficient government services while maintaining citizen control over personal data [73].


### 2.3 People-First AI and Human Complementarity

The discourse surrounding AI's societal impact is frequently polarized between uncritical enthusiasm—the belief that technology will automatically solve complex social problems—and dystopian alarm—the conviction that intelligent machines will inevitably displace human workers and concentrate power among technological elites [74]. Neither pole adequately captures the nuanced reality of AI's potential, and neither offers a productive foundation for policy. A more constructive approach centers on the principle of human complementarity: the deliberate design of AI systems to augment, enhance, and extend human capabilities rather than to replace or diminish them [75].

Human-Complementary AI begins with the recognition that human intelligence and machine intelligence possess fundamentally different strengths. Humans excel at contextual judgment, ethical reasoning, creative problem-solving, empathetic communication, and the navigation of ambiguous or novel situations. Machines excel at processing vast quantities of data, identifying patterns invisible to human perception, performing repetitive tasks with unwavering consistency, and operating at speeds that biological cognition cannot match [76]. The most productive applications of AI arise at the intersection of these complementary capabilities—where machines handle the computational heavy lifting while humans provide oversight, interpretation, and value alignment [77].

Promoting human-complementary approaches requires deliberate policy intervention because market incentives alone tend to favor labor-replacing applications that reduce costs over labor-augmenting ones that enhance productivity while preserving employment [78]. Public research and development funding can be directed toward AI applications that explicitly aim to enhance human workers rather than eliminate them—diagnostic tools that assist physicians rather than replace them, pedagogical systems that support teachers rather than substitute for them, agricultural advisors that complement farmers' local knowledge rather than override it [79]. Smart procurement policies can give preference to AI solutions that demonstrate human-complementary design, creating market incentives for inclusive innovation [80].

The warning against techno-solutionism carries particular weight in development contexts [81]. The deployment of AI in education, healthcare, or agriculture in low-resource settings often proceeds from an implicit assumption that technology can compensate for systemic deficits—that an AI tutoring system can substitute for qualified teachers, or that a diagnostic algorithm can replace trained clinicians. Experience consistently demonstrates the fallacy of this assumption [82]. AI operates effectively only when embedded within supportive ecosystems that include trained personnel who understand its capabilities and limitations, localized content that reflects community contexts and languages, institutional processes that integrate technological tools into existing workflows, and feedback mechanisms that enable continuous improvement. A people-first strategy recognizes that AI serves human well-being and safety at its core, and that technology divorced from human capacity and institutional support produces neither efficiency nor equity [83].

## Section 3: AI in Practice — Driving Inclusive Business and Societal Growth

### 3.1 AI for Accelerating the SDGs

The Sustainable Development Goals represent humanity's most comprehensive articulation of shared aspirations for a just and prosperous world. Achieving these ambitious targets by their intended timeline demands acceleration beyond what conventional approaches can deliver. Artificial intelligence, when deployed thoughtfully and equitably, offers a powerful instrument for fast-tracking progress across multiple SDG domains simultaneously [84]. The key lies in ensuring that AI applications are designed with development objectives explicitly in mind, rather than expecting general-purpose commercial systems to produce inclusive outcomes as incidental byproducts.

In the domain of food security and sustainable agriculture, corresponding to SDG 2, AI applications are demonstrating remarkable potential to enhance smallholder productivity while reducing environmental impact. Precision agriculture systems that combine satellite imagery, soil sensors, and weather data with machine learning algorithms can provide individualized planting recommendations, early pest and disease warnings, and optimized irrigation schedules to farmers who previously relied solely on traditional knowledge and intuition [85]. Mobile-based advisory services powered by AI can deliver this guidance in local languages through simple interfaces accessible even on basic smartphones. In East Africa, AI-driven crop monitoring systems have enabled farmers to identify pest infestations days before visible symptoms appear, allowing early intervention that preserves yields.

Educational transformation aligned with SDG 4 represents another frontier where AI is accelerating progress [86]. Intelligent tutoring systems can adapt to individual learning pace and style, providing personalized instruction that would be impossible for a single teacher managing dozens or hundreds of students simultaneously. Language learning applications powered by natural language processing can serve speakers of minority languages who lack access to qualified instructors. Automated assessment tools can provide immediate feedback at scale, freeing teachers to focus on mentorship, motivation, and the cultivation of higher-order thinking skills that machines cannot replicate. In refugee camps and conflict-affected regions, AI-powered educational platforms have maintained learning continuity for displaced children who would otherwise lose years of schooling.

Healthcare applications corresponding to SDG 3 showcase AI's capacity to extend specialized expertise to underserved populations [87]. Diagnostic algorithms trained on medical imaging can identify conditions such as diabetic retinopathy, tuberculosis, and certain cancers with accuracy comparable to specialist physicians—bringing diagnostic capability to clinics in rural areas where such specialists are entirely absent. Predictive models analyzing community health data can identify disease outbreaks before they escalate, enabling preventive interventions that save lives and reduce costs. AI-powered triage systems can help community health workers prioritize cases and make appropriate referral decisions, functioning as force multipliers that extend the reach of scarce healthcare professionals.

Financial inclusion aligned with SDG 8 has been transformed by AI's capacity to assess creditworthiness through alternative data sources—mobile phone usage patterns, utility payment histories, social network characteristics—that provide insights into individuals who lack the formal financial records that traditional lending institutions require [88]. This capability has opened access to credit, insurance, and savings products for millions of previously excluded individuals, enabling entrepreneurship, risk management, and asset accumulation among populations that conventional financial systems deemed unservable.

### 3.2 AI and the Future of Work

The relationship between artificial intelligence and employment constitutes one of the most consequential—and contested—dimensions of the inclusive growth agenda. Projections suggest that up to forty percent of jobs globally could be significantly affected by AI-driven automation within the coming decade, though the nature of this impact varies enormously across sectors, skill levels, and geographies [7]. Understanding this variation is essential to designing policy responses that protect vulnerable workers while harnessing AI's productivity-enhancing potential.

The distributional implications of AI-driven workplace transformation differ sharply between developed and developing economies. In wealthy nations, AI primarily threatens middle-skill routine jobs—clerical work, basic data analysis, standardized customer service—while simultaneously creating demand for high-skill technical roles and augmenting productivity in knowledge-intensive occupations [75]. In developing economies, the picture is more complex. Manufacturing jobs that provided pathways out of poverty for previous generations may be automated before sufficient alternative employment emerges [78]. Agricultural communities face displacement as precision farming reduces labor requirements. Yet AI also creates new categories of work—data annotation, AI system training, digital service delivery—that can provide income opportunities if workers possess the necessary skills.

The critical imperative is re-skilling and up-skilling workforces at unprecedented speed and scale. Traditional educational institutions, with their multi-year degree programs and slow curriculum revision cycles, cannot alone meet this challenge. Complementary approaches include modular credentialing systems that allow workers to acquire specific competencies without committing to full degree programs, employer-provided training that integrates skill development into workplace routines, and government-funded transition programs that support workers moving between declining and growing sectors [63]. Digital literacy programs must reach beyond youth populations to encompass mid-career workers who may be most vulnerable to displacement yet least prepared for digital transformation.

Designing AI tools in genuine collaboration with the workers who will use them—rather than imposing systems designed by engineers unfamiliar with workplace realities—produces better outcomes on multiple dimensions [89]. Workers possess tacit knowledge about their tasks, workflows, and challenges that no external observer can fully replicate. Participatory design processes that treat workers as expert informants rather than passive recipients of technology generate tools that are more effective, more readily adopted, and less likely to produce unintended consequences. Moreover, collaborative design processes build worker agency and ownership, reducing the fear and resistance that often accompany technological change imposed from above.

Preventing brain drain from developing countries requires creating conditions in which talented individuals can pursue rewarding AI-related careers without emigrating [28]. This demands investment in local research institutions, competitive compensation structures supported by both public and private funding, and professional ecosystems that offer intellectual stimulation, career advancement, and meaningful impact. Regional centers of excellence that concentrate resources and talent can achieve the critical mass necessary to compete internationally while remaining rooted in local contexts [29].

### 3.3 Business Models for Inclusion and New Markets

The business case for inclusive AI rests on a straightforward proposition: populations and communities excluded from current AI systems represent not merely a social equity concern but also an enormous untapped market opportunity [21]. Companies that develop AI solutions capable of serving linguistically diverse, economically marginalized, or geographically remote populations access customer bases that competitors focused solely on affluent markets overlook. This framing transforms inclusion from a charitable obligation into a strategic imperative, aligning profit motives with development objectives in a mutually reinforcing dynamic.

Linguistic inclusion exemplifies this convergence of business opportunity and social equity. The majority of the world's population speaks languages poorly served by existing AI systems [32]. Natural language processing, voice recognition, and conversational AI remain overwhelmingly optimized for a handful of high-resource languages—primarily English, Mandarin, and a few major European languages. Companies that invest in developing AI capabilities for underserved languages—Swahili, Hindi, Yoruba, Quechua, Bengali, and hundreds of others—gain first-mover advantage in markets comprising billions of potential users. This investment simultaneously advances inclusion by enabling speakers of these languages to access information, services, and economic opportunities previously available only to those who command dominant languages.

Homegrown innovation—AI development rooted in local contexts, addressing local challenges, and leveraging local knowledge—offers advantages that imported solutions cannot replicate [36]. Local developers understand the nuances of domestic markets, cultural sensitivities, regulatory environments, and infrastructure constraints in ways that distant technology companies cannot. Supporting local AI ecosystems through incubators, venture funding, mentorship networks, and market access programs generates solutions with superior contextual fit while building domestic capacity and retaining economic value within communities. The resulting innovations may also prove globally relevant, as solutions designed for resource-constrained environments often pioneer efficiency and accessibility features valued by users worldwide.

## Section 4: Governance, Collaboration, and the Path Forward

### 4.1 National AI Strategies: A Whole-of-Government Approach

The governance of artificial intelligence cannot be left to market forces alone. National governments bear primary responsibility for establishing the regulatory frameworks, institutional arrangements, and strategic investments that determine whether AI serves broad public interests or narrow private ones [35]. Effective national AI strategies require a whole-of-government approach—coordinating efforts across ministries, regulatory bodies, educational institutions, and economic development agencies—because AI's impacts cut across every sector and policy domain simultaneously.

The proliferation of national AI strategies in recent years reflects growing recognition of this imperative. Dozens of countries have published formal AI strategies, though the quality, comprehensiveness, and implementation capacity behind these documents varies enormously [24]. Wealthy nations with established technology sectors tend to produce strategies focused on maintaining competitive advantage, attracting talent, and promoting industrial applications. Developing countries face a different strategic challenge: they must simultaneously build foundational capacity—infrastructure, skills, institutional frameworks—while positioning themselves to capture AI's developmental benefits rather than merely consuming technologies designed elsewhere [36].

The urgency for developing countries to articulate their own distinctive strategic paths cannot be overstated. Copying strategies designed for economies with abundant capital, established research universities, deep talent pools, and mature regulatory institutions produces documents disconnected from local realities [51]. A country with limited electricity coverage cannot meaningfully implement a strategy premised on ubiquitous cloud computing. A nation with few PhD-level researchers cannot adopt a strategy centered on frontier research. Effective strategies must begin from an honest assessment of existing capabilities, constraints, and comparative advantages, then chart pragmatic pathways toward building the capacity necessary for progressively more sophisticated AI engagement.

A whole-of-government approach demands coordination mechanisms that break down traditional bureaucratic silos. AI policy intersects with economic policy, social policy, infrastructure policy, and governance policy [46]. No single ministry possesses the mandate or expertise to address all these dimensions. Inter-ministerial committees, dedicated AI coordination bodies, or empowered chief AI officers can provide the strategic coherence necessary to align disparate initiatives toward shared objectives.

Regulatory modernization constitutes a critical component of national strategy. Existing regulatory frameworks—designed for analog economies and traditional business models—frequently prove inadequate for governing AI systems that operate at machine speed, learn and evolve continuously, and produce decisions through processes that resist human interpretation [69]. Updating data governance regulations to address AI-specific challenges such as training data provenance, algorithmic transparency, and automated decision-making is essential. Privacy frameworks must be calibrated to protect individuals from AI-enabled surveillance and profiling without imposing compliance burdens so onerous that they stifle beneficial innovation [71].

### 4.2 Global Collaboration and Multi-Stakeholder Governance

No nation can govern artificial intelligence effectively in isolation. AI systems cross borders effortlessly—trained on globally distributed data, deployed through cloud platforms accessible worldwide, and producing impacts that cascade across international supply chains, financial networks, and information ecosystems [18]. This inherently transnational character demands international cooperation in establishing norms, standards, and governance mechanisms. Yet the current landscape of global AI governance remains fragmented, ad hoc, and insufficiently representative of the world's diverse populations and interests.

The fragmentation manifests in multiple overlapping and sometimes contradictory initiatives [40]. Various international organizations, regional bodies, multi-stakeholder forums, and bilateral arrangements have produced declarations, principles, guidelines, and frameworks addressing AI governance. While this proliferation reflects genuine engagement with an important challenge, it also creates confusion, duplication, and governance gaps. More concerning is the systematic underrepresentation of developing countries in these governance processes [46]. The forums where AI norms are debated and standards are set—technical standards bodies, industry consortia, academic conferences, high-level diplomatic summits—tend to be dominated by wealthy nations and powerful corporations, marginalizing the voices of those most vulnerable to AI's exclusionary potential.

Addressing this democratic deficit in global AI governance requires structural reforms to existing institutions and the creation of new mechanisms designed for inclusive participation [49]. An industry accountability framework that establishes binding obligations for technology companies—regarding transparency, fairness, safety, and the distribution of benefits—would counterbalance the current dynamic in which private actors set the de facto rules governing AI's societal impact through their unilateral design choices. Such a framework must include enforcement mechanisms with genuine consequences for non-compliance, moving beyond voluntary principles and self-regulation that have proven insufficient to constrain corporate behavior when profit motives conflict with public interest [48].

A global AI hub for capacity-building, modeled conceptually on institutions like the United Nations Climate Technology Centre and Network, could provide developing countries with technical assistance, knowledge transfer, and collaborative research opportunities that strengthen their ability to engage with AI on their own terms [21]. South-South collaboration represents a particularly promising avenue for inclusive AI governance and development. Developing countries facing similar challenges—limited infrastructure, constrained budgets, shortage of specialized talent—can learn from one another's experiences, share resources, and develop collective bargaining positions in international forums [29].

### 4.3 The 100 Pathways and the Conditions for Impact

The foregoing analysis has established that inclusive AI requires far more than technological innovation alone. It demands infrastructure investment, data democratization, skills development, institutional reform, regulatory modernization, and international cooperation operating in concert [50]. The challenge that remains is translating these insights from analysis into action—moving from understanding what inclusive AI requires to building the practical pathways through which it can be achieved at population scale.

The concept of "AI Diffusion Infrastructure" captures this implementation challenge. Just as technological innovations throughout history required complementary infrastructure for widespread adoption—railroads for industrial goods, electrical grids for household appliances, telecommunications networks for the internet—AI requires a supportive ecosystem of institutions, processes, and capabilities that enable its benefits to diffuse throughout society rather than remaining confined to early adopters and technological elites [51]. This diffusion infrastructure encompasses training institutions that prepare workers for AI-augmented roles, regulatory sandboxes that allow responsible experimentation, procurement policies that create demand for inclusive solutions, financing mechanisms that support AI adoption among small enterprises and community organizations, and feedback systems that capture user experiences and enable continuous improvement.

The ambition of establishing one hundred viable pathways by 2035—repeatable, scalable approaches to moving AI from isolated pilot projects to population-scale deployment—reflects both the urgency and the magnitude of the task [21]. This target acknowledges that no single model of AI deployment can serve the extraordinary diversity of contexts, challenges, and institutional landscapes across the developing world. Each pathway represents a documented, proven approach to achieving AI-driven impact in a specific domain and context: a pathway for deploying AI-assisted diagnostics in district-level health facilities, a pathway for implementing precision agriculture advisory services for smallholder cooperatives, a pathway for establishing AI-enhanced financial inclusion platforms in informal economies.

Building these pathways requires sustained experimentation, rigorous evaluation, and honest documentation of both successes and failures [47]. The tendency to showcase AI pilot projects while quietly abandoning them when external funding expires or technical challenges mount has produced a landscape littered with promising demonstrations that never achieved durable impact. The pathway approach demands attention to sustainability from the outset—building local ownership, developing maintenance capacity, establishing funding mechanisms that outlast initial grants, and designing systems that can be adapted as technology evolves without requiring complete reconstruction.

Ultimately, the trajectory of artificial intelligence is not technologically predetermined [83]. It will be shaped by the deliberate choices that individuals, organizations, communities, businesses, and governments make about how AI is developed, deployed, governed, and shared. The arc of AI's development will bend toward inclusion only through sustained, coordinated effort—effort that recognizes both the extraordinary potential of intelligent systems to advance human welfare and the genuine risks that ungoverned technology poses to equity, dignity, and self-determination. The path forward demands neither blind optimism nor paralyzing pessimism but rather the practical wisdom to build, step by step, the conditions under which artificial intelligence serves not the few but the many—not as a privilege of the powerful but as a shared inheritance of all humanity.
